# The Chronicle of Minaro Autumnalsculpt the Windy — Opus Model

---

## Section 1: Biographical Narrative

### Prologue: Before the Counting of Years

In the age before the reckoning of calendars, when the world of Tar Thran was yet young in its dawning, there was born among the elves of the Oily Gorge a child named Minaro. She came into the world sixteen years before the counting began — in what the chroniclers would later reckon as the year negative sixteen — and she was given in time the epithet Autumnalsculpt, and later still, the Windy. What winds those were, and from what cursed quarter they blew, would not be known for two centuries.

Of her first hundred and nineteen years, the histories are silent. This is not unusual among the elder folk. The elves of the Oily Gorge measured their lives in centuries, not decades, and the childhood of an elf — if childhood it can be called when it spans a mortal's full lifetime — passes in study, in the learning of herb and leaf, in the slow mastery of conversation and the spoken word. That Minaro emerged from this long silence already accomplished in herbalism, in speaking, and in the arts of discourse, suggests a youth spent in patient cultivation beneath the canopy of her civilization's ancient groves.

### Part I: The Poet's Century

It was in the year 103 that Minaro first appears in the records of the world, and she appears as a poet. She changed her vocation from the undistinguished rank of "standard" to that of poet — a title of no small significance among the elves, for whom the composition of verse was regarded as a sacred act, a bridge between the mortal mind and the divine ear.

Her first work, "White Sheen," was composed that same year, and it set the course for what would become eighty-seven years of unbroken devotion to the poetic craft. The following year, she formalized her training by entering apprenticeship under one Lolama Seawaxes, an elder elf woman of some distinction. For the sake of this tutelage, Minaro uprooted herself and became a settler, traveling to be near her master — a sacrifice of place for the sake of knowledge.

For twenty-three years she studied under Lolama's guidance, composing steadily all the while. "Politeness Ancient" and "My Friend Customs" followed in those early years, works whose titles suggest a mind occupied with the rituals and courtesies of elven life. Then came "The Birth of Tongs" and "The Sun Sets on Friendship," poems that hint at the forging of new things and the fading of old bonds. In the year 127, Minaro departed from her master's side and struck out alone. That same year, while praying, she composed a poem — the first of five works that would come to her in states of devotion.

Here the chronicle must note a peculiar contradiction in Minaro's character, for she was known to prefer working alone. Between the years 132 and 148, she denied no fewer than seven aspiring apprentices, each turned away with the same explanation: she preferred solitude in her craft. Yet this same elf who so jealously guarded her isolation would, in time, accept four students of her own. It is a paradox the histories do not resolve — whether she softened with age, or whether certain pupils possessed qualities she could not refuse, or whether the loneliness of centuries eventually overcame her stated preference.

Her apprentices were four: Lene Tresscut, who was in fact older than Minaro herself, having been born twelve years before her in the year negative twenty-eight; Nine Lakevirtue, nearly her contemporary; Thilu Scaleshark, a male elf born in the year 97; and Ale Wardedweather, born in the year 61. She taught them what she knew, and they carried forward her tradition — though not all would live to see it flourish. Thilu perished in the year 145, and Lene in the year 159, their deaths unrecorded in detail but mourned in silence. Nine and Ale endure still.

Through all these decades, Minaro's art was inseparable from her faith. She worshipped the deity Ola with a fervour that suffused her compositions. Eight of her nineteen poems glorify Ola directly, and several were composed in states of prayer or following divine dreams. In the year 132, she dreamed and wrote. In the year 134, she dreamed and wrote again. In 138, she prayed and composed "Ditches Ever Onward." In 144, prayer yielded "The Velvety Ritual." These were not merely poems but acts of worship rendered in verse.

Yet Ola was not the sole divinity to visit Minaro's thoughts. Beginning in the year 156, a second deity — Romi — entered her compositions. Four poems would eventually honour Romi, and two of them were composed while praying. The histories do not record a formal bond between Minaro and this second god, and indeed no official link appears in the records. Whether Romi was a deity she admired from afar, or whether something more mysterious drew her devotions, the chronicles cannot say.

In the year 157, she joined a performance troupe called the Cardinal Clearings, adding to her solitary art the communal practice of shared performance. Her poems continued: "My Friend Manners," "Plait Convenient," "Stunts," "The Constructive Age and Sacrifice." Then "The Birth of Fidelity" in the year 174, composed after a dream. Then "Burden and Nothing More" — a title that, in retrospect, reads as prophecy.

Her final poem was composed in the year 190. It was called "Boils and the Floors," and it was written while praying — to Romi, not to Ola. After this, silence. For four years, the poet who had composed steadily for eighty-seven years wrote nothing. The muse had departed, or perhaps something darker was approaching, casting its shadow before it.

For two hundred and nine years, Minaro Autumnalsculpt had lived a life of peace. Not one violent act mars the record. Not one battle, not one blow struck in anger. She was a poet, an herbalist, a teacher despite herself, a devotee of gods who spoke to her in dreams. She had mastered the arts of conversation and speaking to their highest attainable levels. She had lost students and left masters. She had wandered the groves and composed her verse.

Then the year 194 arrived, and the world broke.

### Part II: The Bite

The one who destroyed Minaro's life was a dwarf named Kogsak Claspswim, called the Dented. He was a werebeast — a creature cursed by a deity to transform into a monstrous bull, a thing of horn and fury. How a dwarf came to encounter an elf poet in the year 194, the histories do not explain. What they record is this: Kogsak bit Minaro. He bludgeoned her. She was wounded, and she lost the fight.

But she did not die.

What she lost was worse than her life. The bite of Kogsak carried within it the curse of the werebull — a divine affliction, transmitted through blood and saliva, that would henceforth dwell within Minaro's body like a second soul. The scholarly notation is clinical: DEITY_CURSE_WEREBEAST_BULL_BITE. The reality was an abomination. The elf who had spent two centuries perfecting verse and cultivating herbs now carried within her the capacity to transform into a raging bull-creature, a thing of muscle and horn and indiscriminate violence.

She was two hundred and ten years old.

The following year, 195, Minaro became a wanderer. The reason recorded is a single word: flight. She fled to the coordinates 198,103 — a location unmarked by any settlement name in the records, some nameless place at the edge of the mapped world. The poet who had once traveled to be near her master now traveled to be far from everyone. Whether this was shame, or fear of what she might become, or the blind panic of a wounded animal, the chronicles do not say. But for thirty-one years she would wander, and in that wandering she would become something unrecognizable.

### Part III: The Rampage

Three years of wandering passed before Minaro found — or was found by — the Society of Creams, an outcast band. In the year 198, she was appointed their Master. That an elf poet, freshly cursed and newly fled, could rise to command an outcast group within three years speaks to qualities the poetry records do not capture: her skills in intimidation and lying, in intrigue and negotiation, were considerable and may have predated the curse. Or perhaps the curse itself conferred a terrible authority.

It was the following year, 199, that Minaro Autumnalsculpt killed for the first time.

Her victim was Manba Larkcouples, a human female. Manba had been born in the year 198. She was approximately one year old.

The kill was made in werebull form. The poet, the herbalist, the devotee of Ola — she had transformed into a bull-creature and killed an infant. Whatever remained of the elf who had composed "My Friend Customs" and "The Birth of Fidelity" must have been buried very deep indeed.

After this first killing, Minaro became an enemy of two entities. The pattern had begun. Each kill, each attack, would add to the growing roster of her enemies until twenty-one distinct groups would count her among their foes — an exceptionally high number, without parallel in the records of comparable figures.

In the year 201, she attacked two elves — Ula Saffronearths and Iki Basicshins — her own kind, turned upon in scuffles that earned her the enmity of two more groups. The year 202 brought another enemy. The violence was no longer exceptional; it had become periodic, rhythmic, as predictable as the turning of seasons had once been for her poetry.

In 205, she departed the Society of Creams and joined a new outcast band: the Meanness of Omens, where she was appointed Lieutenant. The name alone suggests the character of her new companions.

The year 206 brought a kill of particular significance. Her victim was Mel Shoottwists, a goblin female who was herself a werebeast. In killing Mel, Minaro bit her — transmitting the bull curse onward. This was the first recorded instance of Minaro passing the curse to another living being, and though Mel died of the encounter, the act itself revealed something new: Minaro was not merely a victim of the curse. She had become a vector. The affliction that had been inflicted upon her she now inflicted upon others, a chain of divine punishment extending link by link through the world.

The year 207 saw the death of Gozru Axetick, another goblin, and in that same year Minaro bit Ngoso Chaosseduce, a goblin male who survived and became himself a werebeast. The curse was spreading. In 208, Gozru Amberthieves fell. In 209, two more died: Ozud Fieldplagues — another infant, approximately one year old — and Tode Spideryreined, a goblin male. Both were struck down in werebull form.

Three infant victims across the years. The chronicles record these facts without commentary, but the weight of them is unavoidable. Whatever remnant of Minaro's former self persisted — the poet who prayed to Ola, the teacher who grudgingly took students, the herbalist of surpassing skill — it could not prevent the beast from killing children.

### Part IV: The Long Hunt

The years that followed were a litany of violence punctuated by political manoeuvring. In 214, Minaro bit Eri Questhoppers, a human woman who survived to become yet another werebeast. That same year brought battles with Nguslu Jackaltan and Amec Urgelearned, and the enmity of three more entities. In 215, yet another enemy was made.

In 221, Minaro killed Tin Matchedteal, an amphibian man — the only non-humanoid among her victims, a creature of the swamps who perhaps never understood what bore down upon him in the form of a raging bull. That battle saw her clash with three additional opponents: Mim Pinesmetals, Nique Reclusesyrups, and Tal Mindfulpants, all of whom survived.

In 223, Olum Cloakgrooved, a human male, fell to the werebull. In 225, Minaro encountered Nguslu Jackaltan a second time — a rematch across eleven years, suggesting either pursuit or the cruel coincidence of crossed paths.

Then, in the year 226, something changed. Minaro ceased her wandering. She became a settler once more and was appointed Representative of the Meanness of Omens. The wanderer who had fled in terror thirty-one years prior had found, or built, a place of power. Her seat was established at Skintame, a town designated site 817, and from there she would exercise an authority that the old Minaro — the poet of the Oily Gorge — could never have imagined.

Yet the killings did not stop. In 230, she slew Sapa Laborstrayed, a human girl of approximately two years. In 231, a new development: Kasmko Bootracks, a human male, confronted her — and she killed him in her elf form. Not as the werebull. As herself. This was the first recorded instance of Minaro killing while wearing her own skin, and the fact that the confrontation was initiated by her victim suggests that by now her reputation preceded her. Kasmko had come seeking the monster, and found that the elf was monster enough.

In 232, she bit Sota Daubflickered, the fourth and final individual to receive the curse from her fangs. Sota survived, another werebeast loosed upon the world. In 233, the hunter Itke Longtreaty attacked her and inflicted the only serious wound of her killing career — a goring that cost Minaro a body part. But she killed Itke in return, again in elf form, and again after the victim had initiated the confrontation. The message of these two elf-form kills is plain: Minaro no longer required the transformation to be lethal.

Her last confirmed kill came in the year 237, when Mebas Luredhandles, a human woman, fell to the werebull. After that, the killing stopped — or at least, the records of killing stopped. The attacks continued: scuffles in 240, a fight with Atthem Floorpoked in 246. New enemies accumulated. But no more deaths are attributed to her after 237.

### Part V: The Monster in Office

Throughout the years of blood, Minaro's political career advanced with a regularity that defies the chaos of her other activities. She was reappointed Representative of the Meanness of Omens in 228, again in 237, again in 242, 244, and 247 — seven appointments in total, a record of periodic reconfirmation that suggests either genuine governance or a leadership no one dared challenge.

That she held the dual positions of Lieutenant and Representative speaks to a consolidation of power within the outcast group. She maintained a seat at Skintame and a concurrent membership in the Prim Coalition, a site government. The wandering poet had become a political figure; the cursed monster had become an administrator.

In the year 247, the most recent event in her records, Minaro was recruited into an intrigue by one Dasmir Bristlespeaks, a human male who was himself a werebeast. The nature of the intrigue is recorded only in its structure: Dasmir as master, Minaro as "general purpose asset," the circumstance being her subordinate position within entity 3969 — the Meanness of Omens itself. A werebeast recruiting a werebeast into a scheme of power. Whatever networks of cursed creatures existed in the dark corners of Tar Thran, Minaro had become enmeshed in them.

### Epilogue: The Windy

At the close of the records, approximately the year 250, Minaro Autumnalsculpt the Windy is alive. She is two hundred and sixty-six years old. She has composed nineteen poems and killed twelve people, three of them infants. She has transmitted the werebull curse to four others, three of whom survive as active werebeasts. She is an enemy of twenty-one entities. She holds positions of authority among outcasts. She has been wounded once, losing a body part, and has survived everything the world has sent against her.

Her skills tell a story of two lives: in one, she mastered poetry, conversation, speaking, and herbalism to their highest possible levels. In another, she became proficient in spear, armour, shield, and dodging — the arts of war she never needed in her first two centuries. She is accomplished in intimidation, lying, negotiation, and intrigue — talents that may have always been latent but that the curse forced into terrible bloom.

She began as a poet who prayed and dreamed of gods. She became a monster who killed children and spread her curse like a plague. She ended as a political figure among outcasts, a werebeast entangled in werebeast conspiracies, still alive, still enduring, still bearing the epithet "the Windy" — a name given, perhaps, in those innocent early years, when the only winds that touched her were the ones that rustled the leaves above her grove.

The gods she worshipped, Ola and Romi, are silent in the later records. Whether they turned their faces from what she became, or whether they watch still, the chronicles do not say. The poet's pen is dry. The werebull's horns are red. And Minaro Autumnalsculpt endures, as elves do, beyond the span of any mercy.
