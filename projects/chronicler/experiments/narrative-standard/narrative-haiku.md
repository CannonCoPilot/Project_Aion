## SECTION 1: BIOGRAPHICAL NARRATIVE

### A Poet's Tragedy: The Life and Curse of Minaro Autumnalsculpt the Windy

In the annals of the world, few lives encompass such extremes as that of Minaro Autumnalsculpt the Windy—a creature of grace and art who became, through no intention of her own, a vessel of destruction and ruin. The chronicles record her span of two hundred and sixty-six years, yet it is the cruel turning-point at her two-hundredth year that shall dominate all memory of her name.

#### The Silent Years and Awakening to Art

For one hundred and nineteen years, Minaro lived as her kind lived—within the safe harbors of elven civilization, unremarkable and unrecorded. Born in the year negative sixteen, an elf of the gentle race, she dwelt among her own without fanfare or distinction. The great chronicles are silent on these early decades, and we must presume them spent in the ordinary pursuits of her people: the crafting of beauty, the tending of forests, the peaceful contemplation of the eternal.

But in the one hundred and third year of the common reckoning, a transformation began—not of body, but of calling. Minaro ceased whatever mundane labor had occupied her hands and gave herself instead to Poetry. This was no casual pursuit. The records attest that she would master the craft of verse with a dedication that spoke to something deeper within her spirit: a hunger to capture truth and meaning through language.

Within a year of her conversion to the poet's art, she sought out a master. In the one hundred and fourth year, she became an apprentice to Lolama Seawaxes, herself a skilled elf of considerable age. For twenty-three years, Minaro studied beneath her elder, composing works while her mentor guided her hand and spirit. Yet Minaro harbored a contradiction within herself—for in those same years, though she repeatedly denied all who sought to study beneath her, claiming that she "preferred working alone," she would eventually take upon herself not one apprentice, but four. This tension between her declared solitude and her actual engagement with the world was never resolved, and it speaks to some deep ambivalence in her nature.

#### The Poetry Years and Devotion

From the one hundred and third year until the one hundred and ninetieth year of the reckoning, Minaro composed nineteen poems. These works, spanning nearly nine decades of her life, reveal the contours of her spiritual obsessions. She wrote of natural wonders and abstract virtues—of whiteness and politeness, of customs and friendship, of fidelity and sacrifice. Twelve of her nineteen works glorified the divine, and eight were dedicated to the deity Ola, toward whom her veneration was absolute.

The poems came often in moments of spiritual heightening: five were composed while she prayed, and three emerged from dreams. The rhythm of her creative life was the rhythm of devotion itself. In the one hundred and twenty-seventh year, she left her master Lolama and struck out to forge her own path, yet her connection to the sacred never wavered. In the one hundred and fifty-sixth year, she began to compose works honoring the deity Romi as well, though her primary worship remained fixed upon Ola.

In the one hundred and fifty-seventh year, she joined the Cardinal Clearings, a troupe of performers, and for a time she walked among those who celebrated the arts in its many forms. The records suggest a woman fully integrated into a community of artisans and spiritual seekers, respected for her gifts, engaged in the perpetual labor of beauty-making.

The four apprentices she trained—Lene Tresscut, Nine Lakevirtue, Thilu Scaleshark, and Ale Wardedweather—would carry forward her legacy. Notably, one of them, Lene Tresscut, was older than Minaro herself, born in the negative twenty-eighth year; yet even one older in years sought instruction from her mastery of the craft. Two of her students would predecease her: Lene in the one hundred and fifty-ninth year, and Thilu in the one hundred and forty-fifth year. But two would outlive her span of years: Nine Lakevirtue and Ale Wardedweather, whose names would be remembered as those who learned from the poet.

For two hundred and nine years, Minaro lived in peace.

#### The Curse Descends

In the one hundred and ninety-fourth year of the reckoning, in an encounter whose exact circumstances are lost to history, Minaro was bitten by a creature named Kogsak Claspswim the Dented, a dwarf of male gender who himself bore the curse of the werebull within his blood. This bite transmitted to Minaro the very same affliction—a supernatural transformation that would make her, at certain hours or under certain stresses, cease to be an elf and become instead a mindless beast of terrible strength.

The moment of the curse is recorded as one of violence: she was wounded in the encounter, struck by bludgeoning force, and she lost that confrontation. Yet she survived, though changed utterly and forever. The records note that she "lost after receiving wounds"—a passive phrasing that suggests both her subjection and her escape.

With the curse came a madness or at least an overwhelming compulsion. In the one hundred and ninety-fifth year, her state changed from that of a settled member of community to that of a "wanderer," and the reason recorded was "flight." She fled to coordinates 198,103, abandoning the comforts of civilization and the company of her people. For thirty-one years, she wandered in the wilderness, a cursed thing cast out from the world she had known.

#### The Monster Emerges

Yet wandering gave way, in the one hundred and ninety-eighth year, to something darker still. Minaro sought out an outcast group called the Society of Creams and, despite her transformed state—perhaps because of it—rose to the position of Master among them. In that year, at age one hundred and ninety-eight, she killed for the first time: a human infant named Manba Larkcouples, born only the previous year, struck down by Minaro in her werebull form. The child had barely experienced life when it was taken.

The killing opened a door that would never close. Over the next thirty-nine years, through the remainder of her existence as recorded in these chronicles, Minaro would kill eleven more times, with intervals sometimes of mere years between the murders. The victims came from many peoples: goblins, humans, and once an amphibian being. Most were slain in her werebull form, transformed and bestial, acting from compulsion or hunger that she could not name. Yet twice—in the two hundred and thirty-first year against Kasmko Bootracks, and in the two hundred and thirty-third year against Itke Longtreaty—she killed in her natural elf form, suggesting that the boundary between the person she had been and the monster she had become had grown permeable and uncertain.

Among her victims were three children: Manba at one year old, Ozud Fieldplagues at one year old, and Sapa Laborstrayed at two years of age. The records do not speculate on whether Minaro took these lives deliberately or in blind rage, whether she chose these small creatures or whether they simply stood before her at the moment of her transformation. Yet the fact remains: she killed children.

In the two hundred and thirty-third year, Minaro herself suffered wounding—the only recorded instance of her own blood being spilled in violence. Itke Longtreaty gored her, and she lost flesh and bone from her body. Whether this injury humbled her or merely enraged her further, the records do not say, but she killed Itke in return, in her elf form, and the wound seems not to have diminished her capacity for destruction.

#### The Curse Spreads

Yet Minaro was not merely a destroyer; she became a vector for the very curse that consumed her. In four documented instances, she bit others and transmitted the werebull affliction to them. The first was Mel Shoottwists, a goblin female and werebeast herself, whom Minaro bit in the two hundred and sixth year; Mel did not survive the encounter. The second was Ngoso Chaosseduce, a goblin male bitten in the two hundred and seventh year, who survived and became a werebeast of his own, joining the cursed company that walked the world. The third was Eri Questhoppers, a human female bitten in the two hundred and fourteenth year, who survived and joined the ranks of the transformed. The fourth was Sota Daubflickered, a human male bitten in the two hundred and thirty-second year, who also survived.

Three of these newly-cursed beings lived on, spreading their own contamination further and creating a network of transformed creatures. Minaro was no longer a lone monster; she had become the progenitor of an entire cadre of werecursed beings.

#### The Political Ascendancy

Yet the strangest chapter of Minaro's chronicle remains her rise to prominence among the outcast societies and governmental bodies of the lands. In the two hundred and fifth year, she left the Society of Creams and took on the position of Lieutenant in an outcast faction called the Meanness of Omens. In the two hundred and twenty-sixth year, after wandering for three decades, she was formally appointed Representative of that same outcast group.

From that year onward, she was reappointed to the Representative position again and again: in the two hundred and twenty-eighth year, the two hundred and thirty-seventh, the two hundred and forty-second, the two hundred and forty-fourth, and the two hundred and forty-seventh. Seven times across two decades, she was elevated to represent the very outcasts and monsters among whom she dwelt. The world had transformed its perception of her—or perhaps she had transformed herself—from monster to leader.

Her seat of power was established at Skintame, a town that became synonymous with her name. There, a cursed werebeast served as representative to an outcast faction, wielding authority and conducting the affairs of those whom conventional society had cast aside. It is a vision of the inverted world: those rejected by the normal order finding structure and hierarchy among themselves, with Minaro at the apex.

In the two hundred and forty-seventh year, the final recorded event in her long chronicle, Minaro was recruited into an intrigue network by Dasmir Bristlespeaks, himself a werebeast and holder of authority. She was designated a "general purpose asset," a tool to be deployed in the service of the outcast faction's mysterious stratagems. The werebeast network consolidated its power through her, binding curse-bearer to curse-bearer in pursuit of aims known only to them.

#### The Tragedy Embodied

When the chroniclers close their books, Minaro Autumnalsculpt the Windy remains alive, still existing at the approximate age of two hundred and sixty-six years. She has lived longer than most mortals could imagine, witnessed more epochs than any single being ought to endure. Yet this longevity is not a blessing; it is the prolongation of a curse.

A creature who devoted the first two centuries of her existence to the creation of beauty and the worship of the divine was transformed, against her will and through no fault of her own, into a being of violence and destruction. A poet who had mastered the subtle arts of language and spiritual devotion became a monster who could not control the rages and compulsions that seized her form. A solitary artist who denied students yet trained four became a leader among the desperate and the damned.

The tragedy lies not in her death—she does not die—but in the inversion of everything she was. The records measure her prominence score at the maximum possible: 1.0. She has achieved an eminence that no artist, no devotee, no apprentice-master could have attained through art and piety alone. Instead, it came through blood and curse and the strange mercy of those who recognized in her something worth preserving.

One can only imagine, in the silence between the lines of these dry facts, the internal torment of a being who remembers two hundred years of peace and now must live with twelve murders upon her conscience—and the knowledge that, should the curse seize her again, there may be more.

The Chronicle of Minaro Autumnalsculpt the Windy is a tragedy not because it ends in death, but because it ends in transformation. She is alive, yes. But the poet who wrote of white beauty and ancient politeness is gone, replaced by something that wears her form and bears her name but speaks only in violence.
