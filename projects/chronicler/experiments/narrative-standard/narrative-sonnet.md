## Section 1: Biographical Narrative

### The Chronicle of Minaro Autumnalsculpt the Windy: Poet, Devotee, Cursed

*Compiled from the Annals of the World, being a faithful account of the life of one Minaro Autumnalsculpt, she who is called the Windy, an elf of the Oily Gorge civilization, whose years have spanned from the sixteenth year before the reckoning unto the present age — a span of two hundred and sixty-six years — and whose fate stands as testament to the cruelty that fortune may visit upon even the most peaceable of souls.*

---

Let it be known to all who read these pages that the elven people count their centuries as mortals count their decades, and so the life herein recorded must be understood against the long rhythms of elvenkind. What passes swiftly in the telling endured, in truth, across generations of shorter-lived races. Minaro Autumnalsculpt was born in the sixteenth year before the world's common reckoning. She was, by every measure available to the chronicler's art, a creature of peace.

For one hundred and nineteen years, no record survives of any deed that demanded the attention of history. She dwelt, we may presume, within the old places of her people, among the groves and clearings claimed by the Oily Gorge, learning what all young elves learn: the names of plants, the cadence of speech, the shapes of cloud and season. That she came in time to supreme skill in herbalism tells us she walked with her hands in the earth for long years before anyone thought her worthy of a scribe's notice. That she achieved equal mastery in conversation and in speaking tells us she was one who understood her people deeply. She was, in the simplest summation, a forest creature growing toward the light.

Then, in the one hundred and third year of the common reckoning — when Minaro was already one hundred and nineteen years of age — history first turned its eye upon her. She changed her profession to that of poet. This is the first recorded fact of her life: not a battle, not a marriage, not a founding or a conquering, but a quiet decision about how to spend one's hours. She became a poet.

Within a year she sought instruction, and found it in the person of Lolama Seawaxes, a fellow elf who would serve as her master for some twenty-three years. Let the reader note this detail, for it casts a long shadow: Minaro Autumnalsculpt, who would later spend decades turning away those who begged to learn from her, once herself sat at another's feet. She understood what it meant to be the supplicant. She had drunk from the well of generosity. Whatever drew her in later years to refuse the same gift to others was not ignorance of its value.

Under Lolama's tutelage she grew into her craft. When she left her master in the one hundred and twenty-seventh year, she had already begun composing the poems that would define her legacy. The first of these, "White Sheen," emerged from those early years — a title that conjures the quality of light through leaves, the shimmer of snowmelt in a season's first warmth. She was, by this time, a woman of her calling.

The decades that followed were years of devotion — to Ola, the deity she worshipped, and to the discipline of verse. She composed poems while deep in prayer, the words arriving as gifts from whatever power she served. She composed poems after dreams. There is something holy in this practice: the poet who works not from ambition but from attentiveness, whose instrument is receptivity rather than will. Eight of her poems were written in glorification of Ola. Four more honored Romi, a deity to whom she prayed though the bond was never formalized. Whatever passed between Minaro and Romi existed outside official record — a private devotion, perhaps, or a love too quiet for the world's accounting.

In the one hundred and fifty-seventh year she joined a performance troupe called the Cardinal Clearings. The chronicler imagines, though cannot prove, that this was a period of rare sociability for her. She who preferred solitude stepping into ensemble. The troupe would not hold her long, but the joining tells us that whatever preference for solitude she carried, it did not harden into misanthropy. She could be moved to gather with others. She simply did not seek it.

Consider the strange tension at the heart of her middle years. She refused apprentices. Seven times, across seventeen years — in the one hundred and thirty-second year, the one hundred and thirty-third, the one hundred and forty-third, the one hundred and forty-eighth, and beyond — she turned them away. The record notes, with the peculiar precision that only history can muster, that she did so because she preferred working alone. And yet. The woman who refused seven supplicants eventually took four apprentices. Four students shaped by her hand, her voice, her understanding of what verse could accomplish. One of them, Lene Tresscut, was older than Minaro herself — born even earlier in the long reckoning, a reminder that age among elves is not the hierarchy it is among mortals. Perhaps it was Lene's particular need, or Lene's particular gift, that finally overcame Minaro's preference for solitude. Perhaps the poet simply reached a point where she understood that art which is not transmitted is art that dies.

Two of those four apprentices preceded Minaro in death. Thilu Scaleshark fell in the one hundred and forty-fifth year. Lene Tresscut followed in the one hundred and fifty-ninth. The chronology is pitiless: Minaro outlived students who outlived students of their own. The long life of elves is not always gift.

Throughout these centuries the poetry continued. Nineteen works in total, composed across eighty-seven years, from the one hundred and third to the one hundred and ninetieth. The titles accumulate like a catalog of a particular sensibility: "My Friend Customs," "The Sun Sets on Friendship," "And We Sang 'Ruthlessness!'," "The Birth of Fidelity," "Burden and Nothing More." A reader with patience might trace in these titles a portrait of a mind: one interested in the intersection of social bonds and the passage of time, in the way things come into being and the way they end. The poems about birth — of tongs, of vestibules, of brightnesses — speak to a maker's delight in origins. The poems about sunsets speak to her awareness of conclusion.

The last poem she ever wrote was called "Boils and the Floors." It was composed in the one hundred and ninetieth year, while she prayed to Romi. Then silence. No further words. The chronicler who follows her life looking for the next composition will search in vain. The muse did not return, or if it did, Minaro was no longer capable of receiving it.

Four years passed between the final poem and the catastrophe.

---

In the one hundred and ninety-fourth year of the common reckoning, in the two hundred and tenth year of her life, Minaro Autumnalsculpt was found and bitten by one Kogsak Claspswim, a dwarven male who carried within him the curse of the werebull. The bite was not the end of things but the beginning of an ending. She received wounds. She lost the encounter. And she was transformed.

Let us sit with this moment, because the chronicle demands it. Here is a woman who had spent more than two centuries in service to beauty, to verse, to prayer, to the patient instruction of students she only reluctantly agreed to teach. A creature of such profound artistic commitment that she achieved mastery in poetry, in speaking, in conversation, in the herbalist's knowledge of the living world. Two hundred and nine years of a life that produced no violent act, caused no death, accumulated no enemies. And then a dwarf's bite in some unrecorded place, and all of it undone.

The curse of the werebull is not a metaphor in these records. It is a documented, propagating affliction. Once received, it cannot be reasoned with or composed away. It does not care for the host's better nature. It is precisely the wrong curse to visit upon someone who prefers working alone — for the werebeast is not alone; it is haunted by an other self, a periodically erupting violence that belongs to no single identity. Minaro did not become a beast. She became two beings sharing one body, and neither one of them would ever be wholly free again.

The following year she fled. The record calls it "flight," and gives coordinates but no company, no direction of movement, no destination that would have meaning to any who knew her. She became a wanderer. She who had spent decades rooted in her people's civilization, composing devotional verse in moments of prayer, now moved through the world as one of its untethered things.

What must those first years of wandering have been? The chronicler cannot enter the interior of a werebeast's experience, but can observe what the record shows: for several years after the bite, no kills are recorded. The poet still existed within the body of the predator. She managed, somehow, to move through a world that now had reason to fear her without — for a time — fulfilling that fear's worst expectations.

Then, in the one hundred and ninety-ninth year, she killed Manba Larkcouples. Manba was a human female, one year old. The kill was accomplished in werebull form.

The chronicler records this without softening it, because the chronicle must not lie: among the first acts of Minaro Autumnalsculpt's transformed existence was the killing of an infant. Whatever the werebull does to its host, it does not preserve the host's preferences. Minaro Autumnalsculpt the poet, the devotee of Ola, the one who worshipped in her better moments and composed verse in her hours of prayer — that creature's hands, or whatever hands the werebull gave her, were laid upon a child. The record does not tell us whether she knew. The record does not tell us whether she wept when the moon passed.

But she survived. And she adapted, after a fashion.

---

In the one hundred and ninety-eighth year, even as she was establishing herself among the outcast groups that would become her world, she rose to lead one such band: the Society of Creams. That a newly cursed, wandering werebeast could achieve mastery over an outcast group within four years of her flight suggests something important: the skills she had spent two centuries acquiring did not abandon her between transformations. She could still speak. She could still negotiate. She could still teach — and if not teach, then intimidate. The intimidation skill that appears in her record alongside her poetry and her conversation, approaching twelve thousand points of investment, was not a gift of the curse. She had cultivated that capacity long before Kogsak's bite.

The years between the curse and the middle of her third century describe a trajectory that might, in another life, have been called a political career. She moved from mastery of the Society of Creams to a lieutenant's position in the Meanness of Omens, then onward to the role of representative — a role reconfirmed again and again, seven appointments across more than two decades. The outcast group that she came to represent granted her a seat of power in the town of Skintame. The wanderer found a seat.

All of this amid ongoing violence. The record is not a redemption arc. The kill count continued: goblins, humans, an amphibian man who appears as the only non-humanoid victim in the twelve total. She passed the curse onward to four others. Three of those four survived and became werebeasts themselves, carrying her particular affliction into the world to propagate further. The curse does not merely destroy; it recruits.

In the two hundred and thirty-third year she was seriously wounded herself — gored, with a part of her body lost in the encounter. She had sustained twelve kills and suffered precious few injuries before this moment. That she survived a wound of that severity, that she returned and killed her assailant even in elf form rather than beast form, tells us something about the person who had grown within and around the curse. The poet's body had become hardened by decades of conflict it had not sought and could not refuse. The skills at spear and armor and shield that appear in her record, approaching ten thousand points each, were not accumulated in a single afternoon.

In the two hundred and forty-seventh year — the most recent event in the record — she was recruited into an intrigue by one Dasmir Bristlespeaks, himself a werebeast, who enrolled her as a general-purpose asset in whatever purposes that network served. The wanderer who became a representative has become an intelligence asset. The arc of two and a half centuries curves in a direction no one who knew the young poet of the Oily Gorge could have predicted.

---

Minaro Autumnalsculpt the Windy is, at the close of the recorded history, alive. Two hundred and sixty-six years of age. Twelve kills to her account. Four curse victims carrying her affliction into the world. An enemy of twenty-one distinct entities. Member of an outcast band and a site government simultaneously. A poet who has not written in more than fifty years.

The chronicle does not moralize. It records. But the recorder, in the solitude of his work, finds himself returning to that last poem: "Boils and the Floors," composed in prayer to Romi in the one hundred and ninetieth year. He does not know what the poem contained. The title suggests something of the texture of lived experience — the painful and the mundane juxtaposed, the affliction alongside the merely structural. Perhaps she already sensed what was coming. Perhaps the dreams that had once given her poems now showed her images she did not wish to transcribe.

Or perhaps it was merely the last poem of a poet who had run out of peace in which to write.

Whatever the case, the record stands. One hundred and nineteen years of silence. Eighty-seven years of poetry. One bite in the two hundred and tenth year. Fifty-six years of what follows, with no end recorded and no death anticipated.

The world is long. So, it seems, is she.

*Thus concludes the Chronicle of Minaro Autumnalsculpt the Windy, compiled in the year of the reckoning for the benefit of those who come after.*
