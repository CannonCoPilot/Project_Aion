***Background/Introduction:***  Advancing domestic bioenergy production and reducing dependence on energy-intensive synthetic fertilizers are central priorities of DOE’s biotechnology mission. Achieving these goals requires harnessing the untapped metabolic potential of microbial communities, yet the field faces a fundamental barrier: genetic potential routinely fails to translate into realized function, and the emergent phenotypes of multi-species consortia remain beyond our predictive reach. Revolutionary discoveries enabled by microbial enzymes and consortia are possible, but only if we can accurately predict and manage the realized phenotypes of individual organisms in diverse assemblages.  
Decades of traditional microbiological research have identified many of the genes and pathways involved in important microbially-mediated phenotypes such as nitrogen fixation. However, genetic potential often fails to translate into realized function in microbial systems as environmental conditions change, organisms compete, and genes go unexpressed. The advent of high throughput sequencing technologies and multi-omics approaches can help bridge the gap between genotype and realized phenotype with microbial communities but these data streams create new challenges. Technological advances now allow data to be generated far more rapidly than it can be comprehensively analyzed by human research teams. This has led to large caches of underutilized data and long lag times between data generation and publication. As such, the current state of the field represents a perfect opportunity for artificial intelligence (AI) to leverage large amounts of existing data in order to yield new insights into microbial systems to the benefit of biotechnology, agriculture, and domestic energy production. We propose to build an **Integrated Microbiome Analytic Generator of Interaction Network Evolution (IMAGINE-AI)**. This will be an advanced AI-enabled workflow to understand and optimize genotype-to-phenotype predictions in both pure culture and complex multispecies microbial consortia. The AI system we propose to build will be broadly applicable to microbial genotype-to-phenotype analyses in multiorganisms systems and thus has the potential to advance research and biotechnological innovation in a wide variety of sectors.   
Initially, we plan to focus on microbial consortia that perform biological nitrogen fixation given the significance of this process for bioenergy and food agriculture. The ability to understand, predict, and enhance nitrogen fixation by free-living bacteria (AKA diazotrophs) would revolutionize bioenergy and food crop agriculture. Currently biological N fixation supplies \~20% percent of agricultural N, while \~60% percent is derived from synthetic N fertilizer which is responsible for 1.13 Gt CO2e and causes mass eutrophication in aquatic ecosystems (Ludemann et al. 2024, Menegat et al. 2022, Luna Juncal et al. 2023). Beyond the importance and applicability, diazotrophic N2 fixation is an ideal use case for AI tool development because the genes directly involved in N2 fixation are well characterized but realized rates of strain-specific N2 fixation, and interactions among species in N2 fixing consortia, are currently beyond our ability to predict.   
We envision IMAGINE-AI to integrate publicly available data genomic and phenotypic measurements with novel, high throughput quantitative phenotyping and a cyclical design build test learn (DBTL) workflow. This AI-empowered iterative prediction-experiment loop will provide an AI advantage and accelerate biotechnology innovation. In addition to accelerating science, our work will be directly applicable to understanding and optimizing microbes for enhanced domestic bioenergy and sustainable fuel production. 

**Project Objectives:**  The overarching objective of this work is to build IMAGINE-AI to understand, predict, and optimize realized microbial phenotypes in pure cultures, multispecies consortia, and natural communities. This objective aligns perfectly with the focus topic because it will leverage publicly available databases, multi-omics response surfaces, and environmental drivers to reveal the genomic, biomolecular, metabolic and environmental factors that determine realized microbial phenotypes within microbial communities. Our vision for Phase II is to generate an AI workflow fully integrated with laboratory automation to accelerate discoveries that allow microbiome optimization for desired outcomes such as nitrogen fixation.   
***Phase I Objectives:***   
**A:** Demonstrate an AI advantage in genotype to phenotype predictions of categorical and quantitative microbial phenotypes in pure cultures and consortia. This advantage will include increases in: 

1) genotype to phenotype prediction accuracy (as compared to statistical models) and   
2) the speed and accuracy of scientific discovery. 

**B:** To accelerate our AI-empowered design build test learn (DBTL) workflow in Phase II we will 

1) generate high throughput phenotypic protocols and   
2) establish a collaboration with industry partners specialized in laboratory automation. 

To facilitate both objectives, we will rapidly generate new data on mono and consortia cultures in response to a range of environmental drivers to facilitate modeling of multispecies interactions under changing abiotic conditions.

 **Proposed Research and Methods:** To achieve our objectives, we have assembled a multi-institutional interdisciplinary team with complementary expertise spanning quantitative microbial ecology, AI/ML development, multi-omics, and laboratory automation. At WVU, PI Morrissey will lead overall coordination, data harmonization, and AI model evaluation, drawing on her expertise in quantitative microbial ecology, bioinformatics, and phenomics; Co-PI Romero will contribute AI model development and research computing; and Co-PI Brzostek will lead process-based modeling of organismal phenotypes and AI advantage benchmarking. At Johns Hopkins, Co-PI Choudhary will lead custom AI agent development. At LLNL, Co-PI Pett-Ridge will lead multi-omics data integration and provide access to LLNL computing infrastructure. At PNNL, Co-PI Hofmockel will lead consortia phenotyping and metabolomics.

**AI Integrated workflow (Full team):** All team members will make use of AI agents to accelerate data retrieval, harmonization, and modeling, incorporating linked data frameworks to enable seamless querying of interoperable phenotype databases, thereby reducing manual preprocessing and enhancing data integration, reproducibility, and model performance.     
**Data Harmonization (Morrissey, Kimbrel, and Brzostek):** The development of IMAGINE- AI will begin via the use of publicly available data. Specifically, we will integrate full genomes from IMG, NFixDB, and NCBI with curated phenotype records from BacDive and µGrowthDB. Retrieved genomes will be processed and annotated to generate structural and functional features, which will be linked with corresponding phenotype data to construct a unified phenotype matrix. Initially, we will focus our model development on microbial taxa with fully sequenced genomes and relatively complete phenotype annotations. Pure culture phenotypes from BacDive are primarily categorical (e.g. motility, spore formation, cell shape etc.) while µGrowthDB provides quantitative growth rate phenotypes. Data will be harmonized and split into ‘train’ and ‘test’ partitions.    
**Pure Culture Genotype to Phenotype (Morrissey, Kimbrel, Blazewicz, Romero and Kamal):** We will begin our genotype to phenotype analysis by aggregating genomic traits (genome size, GC content, etc) and mapping protein family domains for each strain’s genome. We will then evaluate the ability of AI approaches (detailed below) to leverage genomic traits and protein families to predict categorical phenotypes. These phenotypes include emergent organismal traits not trivially attributable to single genes such as fatty acid composition, nutritional strategy, and biofilm-forming behavior. In parallel we will develop AI approaches to predict growth rates from genomic information as well as categorical traits. Model outputs will be interpretable and uncertainty-aware, enabling prioritization of predictions for literature review and targeted experimental follow-up. Initial model performance will be evaluated on ‘test’ taxa (not used for model training), after which the framework will be applied to under-described taxa using all available genomic data.  
**Genotype to phenotype in microbial consortia (Morrissey, Pett-Ridge, Hofmockel, Romero and Kamal):** Consortia provide an ideal model system for AI development due to their reduced complexity and ease of experimental manipulation. Once we have a functioning model capable of predicting phenotypes in pure culture, we will challenge it to predict growth phenotypes from in silico generated metagenomes. The predictions will be trained and tested against experimental studies with growth phenotypes for mono and consortia cultures from µGrowthDB and the personal datasets of PI Morrissey and Co-PI Hofmockel. Once the model is predicting microbial phenotypes in consortia we will challenge the model to design consortia for optimal phenotypic performance under different environmental conditions and initiate our first DBTL cycle.   
**Demonstrating an AI Advantage (Morrissey, Pett-Ridge, Romero and Kamal):** To determine if our AI enabled genotype to phenotype analysis demonstrates an AI advantage, we compare our AI model predictions to less advanced, more traditional statistical approaches (e.g. logistic regression, generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) predictions) commonly used for genotype to phenotype prediction (sources). The exact same ‘train’ and ‘test’ data sets will be used for both approaches (AI and statistical) to facilitate meaningful comparison. We will assess not only predictive accuracy but also robustness, generalizability, and performance across phenotypes of varying complexity, particularly those governed by multi-gene interactions. Importantly, our AI framework will leverage genome-derived features, including gene content and protein family annotations, enabling the capture of complex, nonlinear, and multi-gene functional relationships underlying microbial phenotypes. To quantify AI workflow mediated increases in productivity, approximately 5% of tasks (randomly sampled from a pre-specified systematization covering data harmonization, model evaluation, and figure generation) will be performed by both an AI agent and a randomly assigned junior team member, with performance evaluated on (1) time to completion and (2) output accuracy verified by a team member blind to the performer. The time, and the number of attempts, taken to accurately perform tasks will be used to calculate an AI efficiency metric   \= XHuman/ XAI-agent where X \= time(s) or attempts (\#).   
**Generate high throughput phenotypic workflows (Morrissey, Pett-Ridge, Hofmockel, Brzostek):** To prepare for the design build test learn (DBTL) workflow in Phase IIe will develop high throughput phenotyping protocols and generate data to refine model predictions for N2 fixing consortia. As rates of diazotroph growth in nitrogen free media are reflective of N2 fixation phenotypes we will determine growth rates and metabolite production in mono and consortia cultures for bacteria in diazotrophic communities via microplate assays under a range of N-free culture conditions (varying pH, carbon source, oxygen availability, and cofactor availability). Rapid metabolic fingerprinting will be performed via high resolution molecular spectroscopy (Horiba A-TEEM). Targeted transcriptomic and metabolomic analysis of select samples will facilitate interpretation and prediction of microbial network interactions. 

Building IMAGINE-AI in a progression from genotype-to-phenotype prediction in isolates to context-dependent prediction in multispecies systems will establish a scalable framework for understanding how genetic potential is translated into realized microbial function under varying environmental conditions, directly supporting the broader goal of predictive engineering of microbial communities. In Phase 2, we will scale discovery by deploying a modular agentic AI platform linking model predictions to laboratory automation for rapid design-build-test-learn (DBTL) cycles. The validated models, protocols, and team infrastructure established in Phase 1 will provide the essential foundation for this effort.

• **Data Sources and Models** : **Data Sources: Genotypic:** Fully sequenced genomes will be retrieved from the Integrated Microbial Genomes (IMG) platform, the NCBI RefSeq database (\>450,000 prokaryotic genomes; 23,063 species-representative reference assemblies as of 2025), and NFixDB, a diazotroph-specific whole-genome repository containing \>4,000 verified nitrogenase gene clusters across 50 bacterial and archaeal phyla. **Phenotypic:** Traits such as cellular morphology, metabolism characterizations, fatty acid and metabolite profiles will be acquired from the public, curated BacDive database, which contains data on more than 100,000 strains, including 20,060 type strains covering 98% of  validly described prokaryotes. **Integrated:** Additionally we will leverage datasets on isolates and consortia generated by the investigator team that have growth rate phenotypes (n=1420) metatranscriptomes (n=590), and metaproteomes (n=117) as well as fully sequenced genomes and metagenomes for the consortia members. Our planned experimental work is expected to rapidly increase phenotype observations for targeted mono and consortia cultures with fully sequenced genomes.   
**AI Models:** IMAGINE-AI will leverage domain-specific AI models and modern machine learning approaches to capture complex genotype-to-phenotype relationships. Transformer-based biological foundation models such as Evolutionary Scale Modeling (ESM)-style protein and genome language models and AtomGPT-style domain-adapted models will be used to generate learned representations and perform structured reasoning over genomic and multi-omics data. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) will be used to model gene-gene, metabolic pathway, and microbial interaction networks, while multimodal deep learning models will integrate genomic, transcriptomic, metabolomic, and environmental variables. Ensemble approaches such as gradient boosting will be combined with deep learning to improve robustness and predictive performance. To support the design-build-test-learn workflow, we will deploy agentic AI workflows using AtomGPT.org APIs, AGAPI agents, and Model Context Protocol (MCP)-based integrations. These agents will automate data retrieval, harmonization, model training, uncertainty estimation, and experiment planning, while enabling interoperable connections between databases, AI models, and laboratory workflows. Together, these AI models and agentic frameworks will enable IMAGINE-AI to iteratively learn nonlinear genotype-to-phenotype relationships and accelerate microbial consortia optimization.

• **Decision Gate Metrics**:To evaluate our successes for the go/no go decision we will assess the following metrics directly aligned with our objectives.   
**Objective A1)** An AI advantage in the genotype to phenotype prediction will be demonstrated if the AI models

1) Out-perform traditional statistical approaches as evidenced by a statistically significant improvement (permutation test, p \< 0.05) in macro-averaged F1 score (categorical traits) and RMSE (quantitative growth rates) relative to the best-performing baseline (logistic regression or GLMM), evaluated on held-out test taxa using 10-fold stratified cross-validation repeated 10 times. A minimum improvement threshold of ≥5 percentage points in F1 or ≥10% reduction in RMSE will be required for a "go" decision.  
2) Demonstrates scaling behavior wherein, increases in the training set size yield increases in performance. To assess this we will train our model on bootstrapped subsets of increasing size (e.g., 25%, 50%, 75%, 100% of training data), and evaluate whether a significant positive relationship (p \< 0.05, Spearman rank correlation) emerges between performance vs. training set size sensu ref .

**Object A2)** An AI advantage in the speed and accuracy of scientific discovery will be demonstrated if AI efficiency metrics for time and accuracy of tasks are significantly greater than 1 (one-tail t-test, p \< 0.05, across a minimum of 20 randomly sampled tasks).   
**Objective B)** Success criterion includes securing an industry partner and successfully generating data via high throughput phenotyping. Phenotyping efforts will be considered successful if we generate reproducible growth rate measurements with ( coefficient of variation ≤15% across replicates) for at least 30 strains and 3 consortia.
