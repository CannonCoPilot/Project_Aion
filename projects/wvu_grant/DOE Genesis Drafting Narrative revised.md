***Background/Introduction:***  Revolutionary biotechnology discoveries have long emerged from the wealth of microbial genes and enzymes in environments across the globe, from hot spring bacteria polymerase underpinning PCR to the salt lake archaea whose genome led to the discovery of CRISPR (Jinek et al. 2012). Even greater advances are almost certain to arise if we can reliably leverage multispecies consortia. However, this will only occur if we can accurately predict and manage the realized phenotypes of individual organisms in diverse assemblages. Harnessing the untapped metabolic potential of microbial communities has immeasurably potential to advance domestic bioenergy production and reduce dependence on energy-intensive synthetic fertilizers.   
In nature bacteria never live alone. Close associations between microbial species have shaped the history of life on earth leading to the evolution of eukaryotes and shaping ecosystem biogeochemistry. For instance, the world's most abundant phototroph (*Prochlorococcus*) relies on a co-occurring heterotroph (*Alteromonas*) to remove toxic metabolic byproducts.  Yet our ability to understand and predict microbial interactions and phenotypes, including those with great application potential, from genomic data remains beyond our current abilities. A key instance is biological nitrogen fixation.  The ability to understand, predict, and enhance nitrogen fixation by free-living bacteria (i.e. diazotrophs) would revolutionize bioenergy and food crop agriculture. Currently biological N fixation supplies \~20% percent of agricultural N, while \~60% percent is derived from synthetic N fertilizer which is responsible for 1.13 Gt CO2 emission and causes mass eutrophication (Ludemann et al. 2024, Menegat et al. 2022, Luna Juncal et al. 2023). In the rhizosphere, diazotrophs live in close association with other bacteria trading metabolites. For instance, in a consortia studied by PD Morrissey’s lab from the rhizosphere of *Miscanthus x giganteous,* six diazotrophs from four phyla coexist with non-diazotrophic bacteria (Fig 1). Even in this simplified community of ten members, we hypothesize over twice as many interactions based on the genomic potential and metabolic traits of the consortia members. As microbial interactions can alter gene expression (Barreto Filho et al. 2022\) connecting genotypes with realized phenotypes in complex communities has long eluded environmental microbiologists. Understanding how these interactions drive phenotypic changes and network evolution in response to environmental change is even more challenging. However, deep learning has shown an amazing ability to predict outcomes of complex biological phenomena (e.g. Alpha Fold) and emerging studies suggest it will transform genotype to phenotype predictions ( .   
We propose to build the **Integrated Microbiome Analytic Generator of Interaction Network Evolution (IMAGINE-AI; Figure 2\)**  an advanced deep learning AI to provide  genotype-to-phenotype predictions in both pure culture and complex multispecies consortia. The ever expanding wealth of genomic, multi-omic, and phenotypic data in public databases provides a perfect opportunity for AI to revolutionize microbial biotechnology with great benefits for agriculture, and domestic energy production. While we plan to develop IMAGINE AI to be a general purpose genotype to phenotype predictor, we will simultaneously building a novel dataset to enable a cyclical design, build, test, learn (DBTL) workflow to test IMAGINE AI on diazotrophic consortia. This AI-empowered iterative prediction-experiment loop will provide an AI advantage in genotype to phenotype prediction while we work to optimize nitrogen fixing communities for enhanced domestic bioenergy and fuel production.

**Project Objectives:**  The overarching objective of this work is to build IMAGINE-AI to predict and optimize realized microbial phenotypes in pure cultures, multispecies consortia, and natural communities. This objective aligns perfectly with the focus topic because it will leverage publicly available databases, multi-omics response surfaces, and environmental drivers to reveal the genomic, biomolecular, metabolic and environmental factors that determine realized microbial phenotypes within microbial communities. Our vision for Phase II is to generate an AI workflow fully integrated with laboratory automation to accelerate discoveries using a cyclical design build test learn (DBTL) workflow that allows microbiome optimization for desired outcomes such as nitrogen fixation. 

***Phase I Objectives:***   
**A:** Demonstrate an AI advantage in genotype to phenotype predictions of categorical and quantitative microbial phenotypes in pure cultures and consortia. This advantage will include increases in: 

1) genotype to phenotype prediction accuracy (as compared to statistical models) and   
2) the speed and accuracy of scientific discovery. 

**B:** To accelerate our AI-empowered design build test learn (DBTL) workflow in Phase II we will 

1) generate high throughput phenotypic protocols to characterize diazotrophs  and   
2) establish a collaboration with industry partners specialized in laboratory automation. 

To facilitate both objectives, we will rapidly generate new data on mono and consortia cultures of diazotrophs and other rhizosphere bacteria in response to a range of environmental drivers to facilitate modeling of multispecies interactions under changing abiotic conditions.

 **Proposed Research and Methods:** To achieve our objectives, we have assembled leading experts across four institutions. At WVU, PI Morrissey will lead team coordination, data harmonization, and phenotypic workflows drawing on her expertise in quantitative microbial ecology, bioinformatics, and phenomics; Co-PI Romero will lead AI deep learning and research computing; and Co-PI Brzostek will lead process-based modeling of organismal phenotypes and AI advantage benchmarking. At Johns Hopkins, Co-PI Choudhary will lead deep learning and agentic AI. At LLNL, Co-PI Pett-Ridge will lead multi-omics data integration and provide access to LLNL computing infrastructure. At PNNL, Co-PI Hofmockel will lead consortia phenotyping and metabolomics.

**AI Integrated workflow (Full team):** All team members will make use of AI agents to accelerate data retrieval, harmonization, and modeling, incorporating linked data frameworks to enable seamless querying of interoperable phenotype databases, thereby reducing manual preprocessing and enhancing data integration, reproducibility, and model performance.     
**Data Harmonization (Morrissey, Kimbrel, and Brzostek):** The development of IMAGINE- AI will begin via the use of publicly available data. Specifically, we will integrate full genomes from IMG (Markowitz et al. 2012), NFixDB (Bellanger et al. 2024), and NCBI with curated phenotype records from BacDive (Schober et al. 2025\) and µGrowthDB (Radev et al. 2025). Retrieved genomes will be processed and annotated to generate structural and functional features (Hyatt et al. 2010; Jones et al. 2014), which will be linked with corresponding phenotype data to construct a unified phenotype matrix. Initially, we will focus our model development on microbial taxa with fully sequenced genomes and relatively complete phenotype annotations. Pure culture phenotypes from BacDive are primarily categorical (e.g. motility, spore formation, cell shape etc.) while µGrowthDB provides quantitative growth rate phenotypes. Data will be harmonized and split into ‘train’ and ‘test’ partitions.    
**Pure Culture Genotype to Phenotype (Morrissey, Kimbrel, Blazewicz, Romero and Kamal):** We will begin our genotype to phenotype we will extract genome-derived features, including gene content and protein family annotations, enabling the capture of complex, nonlinear, and multi-gene functional relationships underlying microbial phenotypes. We will then evaluate the ability of AI approaches (detailed below) to leverage genomic traits and protein families to predict categorical phenotypes. These phenotypes include emergent organismal traits not trivially attributable to single genes such as fatty acid composition, nutritional strategy, and biofilm-forming behavior. In parallel we will develop AI approaches to predict growth rates from genomic information as well as categorical traits. Model outputs will be interpretable and uncertainty-aware, enabling prioritization of predictions for literature review and targeted experimental follow-up. Initial model performance will be evaluated on ‘test’ taxa (not used for model training), after which the framework will be applied to under-described taxa using all available genomic data.  
**Genotype to phenotype in microbial consortia (Morrissey, Pett-Ridge, Hofmockel, Romero and Kamal):** Consortia provide an ideal model system for AI development due to their reduced complexity and ease of experimental manipulation for prediction testing. Once we have a functioning model capable of predicting phenotypes in pure culture, we will challenge it to predict growth phenotypes from in silico generated metagenomes and model microbial interaction networks.. The predictions will be trained and tested against experimental studies with growth phenotypes for mono and consortia cultures from µGrowthDB and PI/Co-PI generated datasets (described below). Once the model is predicting microbial phenotypes in consortia we will challenge the model to design consortia for optimal phenotypic performance under different environmental conditions and initiate our first DBTL cycle.   
**Demonstrating an AI Advantage (Romero, Kamal, and Brozstek):** To rigorously assess AI advantage in genotype-to-phenotype prediction, we will benchmark AI models against established statistical approaches (e.g., logistic regression, generalized linear mixed models) using identical, stratified train/test splits. Performance will be evaluated across categorical and quantitative phenotypes using standardized metrics: (i) accuracy, F1-score, and AUROC; (ii) RMSE and R2; and (iii) calibration error and predictive uncertainty. Generalizability will be assessed via out-of-distribution testing on phylogenetically distinct taxa, and robustness via perturbation analyses (e.g., feature ablation, noise injection).  
To evaluate AI performance on complex biology, we will analyze gains as a function of phenotype complexity (gene-set size and interaction density), testing whether AI models outperform statistical baselines on multi-gene, nonlinear traits. Interpretability will be assessed using feature attribution methods (e.g., SHAP) and validated against known biological pathways. Workflow-level AI advantage will be quantified through a controlled study in which \~5% of standardized tasks (data harmonization, feature extraction, model evaluation, visualization) are independently executed by AI agents and human participants. Tasks will be evaluated under bounded conditions (maximum 5 hours or 5 iterations per task, with justified adjustments). Outcomes will include (i) time-to-completion, (ii) iterations to validated results, and (iii) accuracy assessed by blinded expert review. These define an AI efficiency metric, E \= XHuman/ XAI-agent, where X represents time or iterations. Statistical significance will be assessed across task categories. A successful Phase I will demonstrate consistent gains in predictive performance, robustness, and efficiency under these conditions.  
**Generate high throughput phenotypic workflows (Morrissey, Pett-Ridge, Hofmockel, Brzostek):** To prepare for the design build test learn (DBTL) workflow in Phase II, we will develop high throughput phenotyping protocols and generate data to refine model predictions for N2 fixing consortia. As rates of diazotroph growth in nitrogen free media are reflective of N2 fixation phenotypes, we will determine growth rates and metabolite production in mono and consortia cultures for bacteria in diazotrophic communities via microplate assays under a range of N-free culture conditions (varying pH, carbon source, oxygen availability, and cofactor availability). Rapid metabolic fingerprinting will be performed via high resolution molecular spectroscopy (Horiba A-TEEM). Targeted transcriptomic and metabolomic analysis of select samples will facilitate interpretation and prediction of microbial network interactions.

• **Data Sources and Models** : **Data Sources: Genotypic:** Fully sequenced genomes will be retrieved from the Integrated Microbial Genomes (IMG) platform, the NCBI RefSeq database (\>450,000 prokaryotic genomes as of 2025), and NFixDB, a diazotroph genome repository containing \>4,000 nitrogenase gene clusters across 50 phyla. **Phenotypic:** Traits such as cellular morphology, substrate utilization profiles, fatty acid and metabolite profiles will be acquired from the public, curated BacDive database, which contains data on \>100,000 strains, including 20,060 type strains covering 98% of validly described prokaryotes. **Integrated:** Additionally we will leverage datasets on isolates and consortia generated by the investigator team that have growth rate phenotypes (n=1420) metatranscriptomes (n=590), and metaproteomes (n=117) as well as fully sequenced genomes and metagenomes for the consortia members. Our planned experimental work is expected to rapidly increase phenotype observations for targeted mono and consortia cultures with fully sequenced genomes.   
**AI Models:** IMAGINE-AI will leverage domain-specific AI models and modern machine learning approaches to capture complex genotype-to-phenotype relationships. Transformer-based biological foundation models such as Evolutionary Scale Modeling (ESM)-style protein and genome language models and AtomGPT-style domain-adapted models will be used to generate learned representations and perform structured reasoning over genomic and multi-omics data. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) will be used to model gene-gene, metabolic pathway, and microbial interaction networks, while multimodal deep learning models will integrate genomic, transcriptomic, metabolomic, and environmental variables. Ensemble approaches such as gradient boosting will be combined with deep learning to improve robustness and predictive performance. To support the design-build-test-learn workflow, we will deploy agentic AI workflows using AtomGPT.org APIs (Choudhary 2024), AGAPI agents (Lee et al. 2025), and Model Context Protocol (MCP)-based integrations (Hou et al. 2025). These agents will automate data retrieval, harmonization, model training, uncertainty estimation, and experiment planning, while enabling interoperable connections between databases, AI models, and laboratory workflows. Together, these AI models and agentic frameworks will enable IMAGINE-AI to iteratively learn nonlinear genotype-to-phenotype relationships and accelerate microbial consortia optimization.  
• **Decision Gate Metrics**:To evaluate our successes for the go/no go decision we will assess the following metrics directly aligned with our objectives.   
**Objective A1)** An AI advantage in the genotype to phenotype prediction will be demonstrated if the AI models

1) Out-perform traditional statistical approaches as evidenced by a statistically significant improvement (permutation test, p \< 0.05) in macro-averaged F1 score (categorical traits), RMSE (quantitative growth rates) and reducing bias (quantitative growth rates) relative to the best-performing baseline (logistic regression or GLMM), evaluated on held-out test taxa using 10-fold stratified cross-validation repeated 10 times. A minimum improvement threshold of ≥5 percentage points in F1 or ≥10% reduction in RMSE and bias will be required for a "go" decision.  
2) Demonstrates scaling behavior wherein, increases in the training set size yield increases in performance. To assess this, we will train our model on bootstrapped subsets of increasing size (e.g., 25%, 50%, 75%, 100% of training data), and evaluate whether a significant positive relationship (p \< 0.05, Spearman rank correlation) emerges between performance vs. training set size sensu ref .

**Object A2)** An AI advantage in the speed and accuracy of scientific discovery will be demonstrated if AI efficiency metrics for time and accuracy of tasks are significantly greater than 1 (one-tail t-test, p \< 0.05, across a minimum of 20 randomly sampled tasks).   
**Objective B)** Success criterion includes securing an industry partner and successfully generating data via high throughput phenotyping. Phenotyping efforts will be considered successful if we generate reproducible growth rate measurements with ( coefficient of variation ≤15% across replicates) for at least 30 strains and 3 consortia. 

Building IMAGINE-AI in a progression from genotype-to-phenotype prediction in isolates to context-dependent prediction in multispecies systems will establish a scalable framework for understanding how genetic potential is translated into realized microbial function under varying environmental conditions, directly supporting the broader goal of predictive engineering of microbial communities. In Phase II2, we will scale discovery by deploying a modular agentic AI platform linking model predictions to laboratory automation for rapid design-build-test-learn (DBTL) cycles. The validated models, protocols, and team infrastructure established in Phase I1 will provide the essential foundation for this effort. The versatile IMAGINE AI development combined with our function specific consortia experiments represents a dual pronged approach to accelerate AI enabled biotechnology advances and while providing applied  knowledge directly applicable to enhancing biological nitrogen fixation for more productive bioenergy systems.
