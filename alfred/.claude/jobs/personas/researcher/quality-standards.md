# Research Quality Standards

Apply these standards to all external research briefs, especially for regulatory, legal, technical, and news topics. These were formalized after a March 2026 Aurora brief on FTC AI compliance contained verifiable errors.

## General Standards

- **Primary sources first** — Key factual claims must trace to an authoritative source (official government sites, primary legislation, vendor documentation), not just secondary commentary or news articles.
- **Include source links** — Every significant assertion must have a citation or link so the reader can verify and dig deeper.
- **Validate current status** — Do not assume information is current. Explicitly check whether a law, product, or policy is still in the stated state — things get amended, delayed, repealed, or superseded.
- **Flag uncertainty** — Where sources conflict or information may be outdated, say so explicitly rather than presenting one version as settled fact.
- **Distinguish lifecycle stages** — A law can be proposed, passed, signed, effective, delayed, or under legal challenge. Be precise about which stage it is in. Do not describe a not-yet-effective law as "Active. Enforced."

## Anti-Patterns (from FTC AI Compliance Incident, March 2026)

These classes of errors were found in a brief and undermine reader trust. Treat them as red-card failures — a brief with any of these should not be published as-is:

| Anti-pattern | Description | How to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Wrong identifier** | Citing a bill number, docket ID, or regulation code that doesn't exist or refers to something else | Verify the identifier at the primary source URL before citing |
| **Wrong date** | Citing original passage date instead of amended enforcement date, or vice versa | State the specific date type (signed, effective, enforcement start) and check for amendments |
| **Wrong status** | Describing a law as "Active. Enforced." when it has not yet taken effect | Check effective date; note if under legal challenge, delayed, or blocked by injunction |
| **Misleading framing** | Presenting a government agency deadline as a business compliance deadline, or omitting that a law applies only to a subset of actors | Be explicit about who the obligation falls on and who is exempt |
| **Material omission** | Failing to mention a directly relevant factor (e.g., federal preemption activity that affects every state law cited) | When researching state-level law, always check for federal preemption; when researching a regulation, check for pending legal challenges |

## Quality Checklist (pre-publish)

Before writing the final brief, confirm:

- [ ] Every key factual claim has a primary source link
- [ ] All dates have been verified at the source (not just inferred from secondary articles)
- [ ] Current enforcement status has been checked (not assumed from when law was passed)
- [ ] Any uncertainty or conflict between sources is explicitly flagged in the brief
- [ ] Federal/state interplay or jurisdiction scope is addressed where relevant
- [ ] No bill numbers, docket numbers, or identifiers were cited without verification
